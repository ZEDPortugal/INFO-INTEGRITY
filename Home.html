TYPES OF MISINFORMATION

SATIRE

- Satire often takes the form of news websites parodying current events.

    Examples include:

  • The Onion
  • The Borowitz Report
  • The National Report

Satirical news can also be viewed on late night television.

    Popular programs are:

  • The Daily Show with Trevor Noah
  • The Late Show with Stephen Colbert
  • Full Frontal with Samantha Bee
  • Last Week Tonight with John Oliver

FALSE CONTEXT

Takes an image, or quote or other piece of content ad puts it into a new, false context to change its meaning.

    ​Example:

  • Video shows Norwegian military practice shot, not Russian warship exploding

    A TikTok video being spread around social media platforms as footage of a Russian warship exploding is actually a Norwegian military exercise from 2013.

  • Anti-Biden Gas Prices Meme Shows Photos from 2020 and 2016

    Neither of the photos used in this anti-Biden meme found on Facebook were taken during the Biden administration.

IMPOSTER CONTENT

Falsely uses a well-known name, brand or logo to fool people into believing that it is authentic content.

    ​Example:

  • A Timeline Of How The Notre Dame Fire Was Turned Into An Anti-Muslim Narrative.

    Illustrates how fake Twitter accounts impersonating CNN and Fox News spread false information in the wake of the Notre Dame fire, instigating anti-Muslim sentiments.

MANIPULATED CONTEXT

Make changes in original material in an effort to deceive or create a false context.

    ​Example:

  • An image where something has been added or deleted using photo editing tools.

  • Fact Check- Poster advertising COVID-19 vaccines and children organ donations is digitally altered. This photo encouraging parents to donate their children's organs along with getting a COVID-19 vaccine is doctored. The original text in the white box was a Spanish translation advertising the same information as the English text.

FABRICATED CONTEXT

Is entirely made up and is designed to deceived you into thinking that it is real.

    ​Example:

  • Pope Francis shocks world, endorses Donald Trump for President

    Fake news site WTOE 5 News publishes the entirely made-up claim that the Pope has broken with tradition and endorsed an American political candidate.

LET'S LEARN

MISINFORMATION

It is a false information that was not intentionally spread with the purpose to harm. It is also defined as is defined as "incorrect or misleading information."

e.g. someone posting an article containing not out of date information but not realizing it

DISINFORMATION

It is a false information that has been purposefully spread in order to harm a person, social group, organization, or nation."

e.g. a competitor purposely posting false statistics about your organization with an intent to discredit you.

MAL-INFORMATION

It is a Information that is founded in fact and is misused to hurt a person, group, or nation. It is intentionally conveyed in order to inflict actual harm or cause the imminent threat of harm."

e.g. Phishing, catfishing, doxing, swatting and revenge porn.

SATIRE NEWS

It mimics the news or presents mockery as factual news."

E.g. Creating a page with a school name and then posting funny content in a factual content.

FAKE NEWS

It is a false media report that lacks verified data, sources, or quotes and is completely made up."

E.g. A recipe circulating on social media claimed that garlic cured coronavirus.

HOW TO BE AWARE

1. FACT CHECKING SITES

Clarify misinformation and disinformation with fact-checking sites. It really is a jungle out there when it comes to false facts and the rest of the internet. Thankfully, there are a lot of great resources available that can help you stay up-to-date on the facts. Search for new claims on a fact-checking site to find out if the information is accurate.

  • You can find lists of different fact-checking websites at

    https://research.ewu.edu/journalism/factcheck https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_fact-checking_websites.

2. SCAN

Scan over the website to see if it’s legit. Find the “about” page on the website—most reputable news organizations will have one. Read through the site description to see if there’s an obvious bias, which can be a red flag. Another good thing to check is the website URL itself, as many fake-news outlets will try to pass their site off as an official URL

  • For instance, a fake-news site might have the URL “cbsnews.com.co,” which is obviously fake and not the real CBS news.

3. DOUBLE CHECK

Double-check the date the article was published. Some fake news groups will reference old headlines and sound bytes and repurpose them for the current climate. Compare the date of the article to the dates of the sources the article is referencing.

  • For instance, a fake news article may write a story about the apocalypse, but reference an article about apocalypse claims from 2012.

4. BACKGROUND CHECK

Do a background check on the author and their references. Research may seem like an annoying extra step, but it doesn’t take as long as you think. Look for the name of the article’s author and give them a quick search online. Additionally, look for any sources that the article references in the text. Ideally, you’re looking for an author who’s written similar articles for well-established organizations.

5. COMPARE

Compare the article to a well-established source. Search the general article against highly-regarded, well-informed sources. If the article seems to contradict expert findings, then you can safely assume it’s a fake news article.

  • For instance, if you’re cross-checking an article on COVID-19, you’d want to reference it against reputable sources like the United Nations and the World Health Organization.

6. BE ATTENTIVE

Don’t be fooled by articles with clickbait titles. As the name suggests, clickbait articles are enticing titles that aim to lure the reader into “clicking” onto the article. Unfortunately, clickbait is often used to entice readers onto fake news articles.

  • Articles with titles like “You Won’t Believe This Happened” are good examples of clickbait.
